EDI Wave 8 Provincial Summary

20 years of EDI Data in BC

Decades of research have demonstrated that children’s first years of development are profoundly important to their lifelong health and well-being. Early childhood development influences physical and mental health, learning and social outcomes over the life course and across generations.

The Human Early Learning Partnership (HELP) has been collecting data on children’s development in BC to “shine the light” on how children are doing and inspire action to promote healthy development and well-being. Wave 8 (data collected 2019-2022) marks 20 years of EDI data collection in BC. This report provides a foundational summary of EDI data across these last two decades, setting the stage for more analysis and research to come.

New to the EDI? Learn more about the EDI, how it's collected and it's impact over the last 20 years on HELP's website.

Wave 8 Highlights

Still 1 in 3

In Wave 8, 32.9% or approximately 13,500 kindergarten children in BC are vulnerable on one or more areas critical to their healthy development. Wave 8 is the fifth provincial data collection in a row where more than 30% of children were vulnerable. Almost two decades of research show that the EDI predicts children’s future outcomes; without additional support and care, children who are vulnerable on one or more areas on the EDI are more likely to experience future challenges in their school years and beyond. With this, the level of vulnerability has significant social and economic cost to BC, not simply as children start school but throughout their lives.

Social & Emotional Development, Areas of Highest Concern

For the third wave in a row, the two EDI scales with the highest vulnerability rates continue to be Emotional Maturity and Social Competence. This is particularly concerning given that these scales measure behaviours that relate to the most common childhood mental health issues. Indeed, these findings are consistent with other studies that show increase incidences of mental health concerns with young children across Canada (Raising Canada).

Geographic Differences

Wave 8 data also continue to reveal large differences in developmental vulnerability rates across regions of BC. At the school district level, vulnerability rates range from #% at the lowest end to #% at the highest. And, in Wave 8, there are only two neighbourhoods with vulnerability rates under 15%, but 23 with rates of 45% or higher. These data provide an important lens on the topography of inequities that continue to face BC children and families.

EDI Data Collection

Interested in finding out More about how the EDI is collected? Click Here

In BC, the Human Early Learning Partnership (HELP, UBC) has been collecting EDI data province-wide since 2001, gathering data for over 330,000 kindergarten children in the last two decades. EDI data are collected on a three-year schedule called a “Wave” to capture sufficient data in all school districts involved in the province. The EDI questionnaire is completed by kindergarten teachers in participating school districts in February of the school year for each of the children in their classroom. Data for over 41,000 children attending kindergarten were collected during Wave 8.

Overall Vulnerability

In Wave 8, 32.9% or approximately 13,500 of kindergarten children were Vulnerable on One or More Scales of the EDI.This rate is similar to the Wave 7 (data collected 2016-2019) rate of 33.4%. This means that there are still 1 in 3 children arriving in kindergarten vulnerable on one or more areas of development important to their future success. The chart below shows the trend for Vulnerable on One or More Scales of the EDI from Wave 2 (2004-2007) to Wave 8 (2019-2022).

There has been a meaningful increase in EDI vulnerability rates over the long-term, rising from 29.9% in Wave 2 to 32.9% in Wave 8. While there was a small decrease in the vulnerability rate between Wave 7, at 33.4% (2016-2019) and Wave 8 (32.9%), this difference does not meet the threshold to be a meaningful change.

Mulitiple Vulnerabilities in BC

The rate for Vulnerable on One or More Scales of the EDI provides a perspective on the overall vulnerability rate for children in BC, but it doesn’t show the complex nature of vulnerability. While many of the province’s children are vulnerable on a single area of development, some are vulnerable on two, three, four or all five scales of the EDI. This suggests that in addition to the increasing rates of vulnerability in the province, the complexity of vulnerability patterns is also increasing. It appears that children and families are experiencing more complex challenges and vulnerable children are experiencing increased struggles across more areas of their lives.

Over long-term, between Wave 2 and Wave 8, the percentage of children vulnerable on two or more areas of development has increased (16.1% in Wave 2 to 19.5% in Wave 8) (Figure #). In Wave 8, this represents over 8,000 children vulnerable on multiple areas of development (two or more scales of the EDI).

New Overall Outcomes

In past EDI data reporting, the focus has been on highlighting rates of developmental vulnerability in populations of children. However, the EDI measures the whole distribution of children’s developmental outcomes, allowing us to understand areas of strength and potential across the five key areas of development as follows. Overall EDI Outcomes

The bar chart above shows the trends over time for the percentage of children in BC on track, in flux and vulnerable on the EDI from Wave 2 to Wave 8. In Wave 8, while almost half of children (45.4%) are on track in their overall development, 32.9% are vulnerable in one or more areas of development and another 21.8% are in flux. The range in rates for “overall vulnerability” is higher over time (4.7 percentage points between the lowest and highest rates) than either “overall influx” (2.2%) or “overall on track” (2.1%). In short, while many children continue to do well across the developmental domains, the increasing prevalence of vulnerability is concerning.

Exploring Deeper: EDI Scales and Subscales

While overall EDI outcomes provide a summary of the developmental health of kindergarten children, more specific information about each area of development can be found at the scale and subscale level. Each scale of the EDI also measures the whole distribution of children’s development, identifying if children are vulnerable, at risk or on track in the specific area of development the scale is measuring. Want to know more about the relationship between the 5 EDI Scales, their subscales and the summary measures of vulnerable, at risk and on track? Click Here .

Scale-level Outcomes

On Track: Children who score above the 25th percentile cut-off score on the scale are on track in the area of development measured by the scale.

At Risk: Children who score between the 10th and the 25th percentile cut-off scores are at risk in the area of development measured by the scale.

Vulnerable: Children who score below the 10th percentile cut-off score are considered vulnerable on the area of development measured by the scale.

Wave 8 Scale-Level Outcomes

The stacked bar chart below, illustrates the percentage of BC children who fall in the vulnerable, at risk and on track categories on each of the five EDI Scales. Most children are On Track in their development on each of the five scales, with 78.4% of children On Track in Language & Cognitive Development, 75.3% in Communication Skills & General Knowledge, 69.8% in Physical Health & Well-being, 68.5% in Social Competence and 67% in Emotional Maturity.

Vulnerability on All 5 Scales of the EDI

Exploring Scale and Subscale-level trends over time, especially long-term trends from Wave 2 (2004-2007) to Wave 8 (2019-2022), can provide insight into which areas of development, in particular, are of most concern. The line chart below shows the vulnerability trends over time from Wave 2 to Wave 8 on all five of the EDI scales. Between Wave 2 and Wave 8, there have been meaningful increases in vulnerability in three of the five EDI scales: Physical Health & Well-being, Social Competence and Emotional Maturity. There has been a meaningful decrease in vulnerability on the Language & Cognitive Development scale, and a stable trend on the Communication Skills & General Knowledge scale over the long-term.

All five scales had stable trends since the last data collection in Wave 7. As in Wave 7, Emotional Maturity was the scale with the highest rate of vulnerability at 17.5%, followed closely by Social Competence at 16.3% in Wave 8. The Social Competence and Emotional Maturity scales are associated. Wave 8 data show that in the province, 8% of children are vulnerable on two EDI scales, and of these, 42.5% were vulnerable on both Social Competence and Emotional Maturity scales combined. The developmental aspects assessed on these two scales represent are indicators of children’s early mental health.

Conclusion

The stable trend in EDI vulnerability rates between Wave 7 (2016-2019) and Wave 8 (2019-2022) is perhaps surprising given the immense disruption caused by the COVID-19 pandemic in BC and around the world. Certainly, data collected through the other Child Development Monitoring System tools (CHEQ, MDI, YDI) during this time tell a different story about the impact of the pandemic on the experiences of children and families (link to article).

The ongoing prevalence of childhood vulnerability, still in 1 in 3, is a continuing concern. As well, many of the youngest infants and toddlers born and developing during the pandemic have not yet entered the school system. As such, the impact of the pandemic on their development may be enduring and will be captured by future EDI data collections.

At the community and neighbourhood levels, there is considerable variability in Wave 8 vulnerability rates and trends over time. The opportunity to explore EDI data at the community, neighbourhood and other regional aggregations will be available soon through a new interactive data dashboard – expected release January 2023.

Overall, there is more to unpack. With Wave 8 marking 20 years of EDI data collection, and a CDMS growing more robust with each year, there is now significant BC-specific child health and well-being data available for analysis and research purposes. This is an exciting time for HELP and our partners in research, government, school districts, and health to explore the data collected and link CDMS data with other administrative datasets to learn more.
